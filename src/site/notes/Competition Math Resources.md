Some of the most valuable resources I used to sharpen my mathematical thinking were the problems and solutions from Art of Problem Solving (AoPS), especially Alcumus and the MathCounts Trainer. These platforms transformed the way I viewed mathematics: instead of seeing it as a school subject focused on memorizing formulas, I began to see it as a scientific field built on patterns, creativity, and discovery.

What made these resources especially impactful was the way I studied with them. Rather than immediately looking at solutions, I challenged myself to spend time exploring different approaches, writing down my reasoning, and identifying where my thinking broke down. When I reviewed solutions, I focused not just on the answer but on the underlying strategy—why a method worked, when it could be applied again, and whether there was a more elegant solution. Over time, this process trained me to approach unfamiliar problems with curiosity instead of hesitation.

I also practiced using past competition papers from the Mathematical Association of America (MAA). These problems were valuable because they recreated the pressure and creativity required in real competitions. I treated them like simulations: working under timed conditions, reviewing every mistake afterward, and keeping a record of problem types that challenged me. Instead of viewing incorrect solutions as failures, I used them as opportunities to discover gaps in my understanding and strengthen my problem-solving instincts.

Together, these resources helped me develop a deeper appreciation for mathematics—not just as a collection of techniques, but as a process of investigation where every problem contains a hidden structure waiting to be uncovered.
